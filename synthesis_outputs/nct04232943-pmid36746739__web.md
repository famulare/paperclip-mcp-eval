## Prompt 1 — Central mechanistic claim?

The central mechanistic claim, as retrievable from the bundle, is **negative**: adding the E. coli double mutant heat-labile toxin (dmLT) adjuvant to intramuscularly administered IPV did *not* augment intestinal/mucosal immunity, did *not* alter humoral (serum neutralizing antibody) responses, and did *not* reduce fecal shedding of bOPV challenge virus relative to IPV alone in IPV-primed healthy adults. The mechanistic framing the study was set up to test — that a mucosal adjuvant co-formulated with a parenteral inactivated vaccine could induce gut immunity sufficient to reduce intestinal replication of an oral challenge virus — is, in this dose/route configuration, *not* supported by the reported results. The conclusion explicitly hedges that the dose used may have been too low ("tolerability of the dose of dmLT used in this study may allow higher doses to be investigated"), so the claim is route/dose-specific rather than a categorical rejection of the dmLT-as-mucosal-adjuvant hypothesis.

## Prompt 4 — What would a domain expert notice that a generic summary might miss?

A polio-vaccine domain expert reading only this bundle would notice several things a generic summary would gloss over:

- **The framing is the canonical gap in polio vaccinology.** IPV→humoral, OPV→mucosal. The dmLT-adjuvant idea is one of a family of attempts to engineer an "IPV + something" that closes the mucosal gap. The abstract's negative result is therefore informative as a *failed instance* of a well-known class of strategies, not as an isolated finding.
- **The challenge model is the key methodological asset.** Day-29 bOPV challenge with 28 days of fecal shedding measurement is the standard polio human-challenge readout for intestinal immunity. The bOPV arm functions as a positive control for shedding-suppression by mucosal immunity; the fact that IPV groups shed more than bOPV recipients confirms the assay was working.
- **The dose-hedge in the conclusion is load-bearing.** "The tolerability of the dose of dmLT used in this study may allow higher doses to be investigated" is the kind of conclusion an experienced reader recognizes as "we likely under-dosed the adjuvant; this is a phase 1 dose-finding signal, not a verdict on the mechanism." A generic summary that says "dmLT doesn't work as a polio adjuvant" would overshoot what the paper actually claims.
- **The route is intramuscular.** Adding a mucosal adjuvant to a parenteral injection is mechanistically aggressive; one would expect intramuscular dmLT to have limited access to gut-associated lymphoid tissue. A domain expert would note that the negative result is consistent with prior expectation about IM-route mucosal-adjuvant delivery and would treat the study as informative about route + dose, not about dmLT-as-adjuvant in general.
- **The author/affiliation composition is the polio-eradication mucosal-immunity consortium.** PATH (Seattle) as sponsor/lead, University of Antwerp clinical site (Van Damme is a known phase-1 polio investigator), ULB for immunology, Dartmouth (Wright, Ackerman) for serology/systems-serology, and CDC Division of Viral Diseases (Konopka-Anstadt, Mainou) for virology.
